# Chapter 4 - Plans

She stepped out of the closet and back into the basement with iron already sitting at the back of her tongue.

The hidden pulse continued.

Press. Pause. Press.

For a moment she let the room hold still around her. The pillars. The cages around the bulbs. The bad arrangement of the shelves. The unreasonable warmth. Nothing looked changed. Everything felt informed.

Her phone was already in her hand.

One bar. Then two. Weak, unstable, usable.

She moved to the shelter of a pillar and placed the call from memory.

It connected on the second ring.

"Well," said a young male voice, polished enough for office work and careless enough to get him scolded for it, "either the building is collapsing or you finally decided you miss me."

"You do enjoy employment as a concept, don't you?"

A quiet laugh.

"Only in the abstract."

"I need a registry pull."

"So this is business. How cruel."

"Address."

She gave it to him. Keys began moving at once.

After a few seconds he said, "Seventeen Saint Casimir Embankment. Baron Marek Vezhin, deceased last week. Old family trust. Expensive security. Enough insurance to survive artillery. Heritage disputes. One amusing complaint from a neighbor who thought the facade looked hostile."

"All of which I know."

"You do like to test whether other people do their jobs."

"Utility plans."

The keys stopped.

"For what?"

"Below-ground service routes. Heating lines. Pipe maps."

Silence answered first.

Then, quieter, "There aren't any."

She looked across the basement.

The stone pillars held. The old pipes crossed overhead. The shelves stood in their ugly, settled wrongness.

"What do you mean?"

"I mean there is no basement on the approved plans." His tone had flattened now, almost fully professional. "The original house burned down. Full loss. The current structure came later. Modern rebuild, too modern for the street, everybody hated it. Ground-level service core. Rear storage. No cellar. No service basement."

She said nothing.

The pulse behind the wall continued.

Press. Pause. Press.

"How long ago was the fire?"

"Forty-one years."

Her gaze settled on the stairs across the room.

One open aisle. A turn past the linen racks. Then the stairwell.

"All right," she said.

"What is this?"

"Nothing you can help with."

"That answer is rarely comforting."

A tall shelf now stood across the aisle she had just measured.

She had not heard it move.

Her fingers tightened around the phone.

"Stay available," she said.

"For what?"

A wooden case dropped somewhere nearby and shattered against the floor.

The call ended in her hand.

She pocketed the phone and moved at once.

The next passage remained open. She took it quickly, feet light over the stone, shoulder turned just enough to spare the line of her blazer. Even in haste there was something neat in the way she moved, as though speed and composure had made terms years ago.

The basement objected.

A hanging dust sheet tore loose and fell across her path. She caught it with both hands, slipped through it in one smooth turn, and came out just as a supply cart rolled from a crossing lane with no one behind it. She pivoted away. Its corner struck her hip hard enough to promise a bruise by evening.

She kept going.

Ahead, between two shelves, the stairs flashed into view.

A mop rack swung out from the side of an aisle. She ducked. One wooden handle glanced off her shoulder. Another tapped her upper arm with a sting more insulting than dangerous. The blows felt chosen for annoyance, as though the room wanted her harried before it wanted her hurt.

She reached the opening where the stairs had been.

Blocked.

Only for a beat. Only by a shelf that now stood at an angle across the route, laden with folded cloth and metal tins, as settled in place as old furniture.

She turned left.

A narrow gap opened between linen racks. She slipped through, one hand brushing the edge for balance, the motion quick and clean enough to resemble dance before it was survival. A small tin fell from above and bounced near her shoe. She skipped it without breaking stride.

The stairs appeared again.

Closer now.

A long wooden case slid from a higher rack and struck the floor ahead of her with a crack that shook the nearby shelving. She checked her pace, stepped over it, and moved on. Another case fell behind her. Then another, farther off. Not random. Herding.

That settled the room's intention.

She ran.

The stairwell stood clear for once, plain and reachable. She took the first steps fast, one hand on the banister, the other held close to keep her balance compact and precise.

One step. Two. Four.

The rise changed under her feet.

She felt it first through the soles of her boots, a soft betrayal in the angle, the staircase loosening its shape before the eye quite accepted it. Then the steps lengthened and ran together into a smooth descending slope.

Her footing vanished.

She dropped onto her backside and slid backward in one quick, graceless rush, heels forward, breath caught high in her chest. The banister blurred beside her. The polished wood burned through the fabric beneath her as the basement pulled her back.

The fall lasted a second.

Long enough.

Her left hand caught the rail. Her right hand went inside the blazer and found the black glove.

No elegance now. Leather snagged at two fingers. Her thumb missed once. She forced it through, dragged the glove home with a clumsy jerk, and had it on by the time she struck the stone floor at the bottom.

She landed hard, seated, one palm braced behind her, hair loosened just enough to spill across one cheek.

Above her, the slope had already become stairs again.

Respectable. Ordinary. Waiting.

She stayed there for one beat of the pulse.

Press. Pause. Press.

Then she rose.

Her hip ached. Her shoulder would color later. Dust marked one side of her blazer. The basement had managed little more than that.

Across the room, the shelves had changed again.

This time there was no effort to conceal it.

Heavy wooden shelves and long storage cases began aligning themselves by inches, row after row drawing inward with deep timber knocks against stone. One lane closed. Then another. A tall unit near the sink straightened. A second answered it. The cases tipped onto their bases with a patient, gathering weight, as though the whole basement were assembling itself for impact.

For a moment she watched them.

Then she looked down at the gloved hand hanging at her side.

Forty-one years ago, according to the records, this basement had already burned once.

The thought steadied her.

"All right," she said softly.

Outside, the mansion stood in damp evening stillness above Saint Casimir Embankment.

Then the small basement windows along the foundation flashed once with blinding blue light.

And went dark.
