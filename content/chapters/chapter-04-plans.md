# Chapter 4 - Plans

She stepped out of the closet and back into the basement with iron already sitting at the back of her tongue.

The pulse behind the wall continued.

Press. Pause. Press.

For a moment she did nothing. She listened, letting the room settle around her again. The broad service level, the old pillars, the badly placed shelving, the wire-caged bulbs, the unreasonable warmth. Everything held its place with that same oppressive composure, as though the blood in the pipe had changed nothing because the room had always known what it was.

Her phone was already in her hand.

One bar. Then two. Enough to risk.

She moved beside a pillar, turned half toward the open floor, and placed the call from memory.

It connected on the second ring.

A young male voice answered, smooth with office polish and already one degree out of line.

"Well, either the building is collapsing or you finally decided you miss me."

She shut her eyes for half a second.

"You say that on a monitored line often?"

"Only when I want to die interestingly."

"You would manage it."

A quiet laugh answered her, practiced enough to stay professional, amused enough to get him reprimanded if the wrong person heard it.

"Good afternoon to you too."

"I need a registry pull."

"So romantic."

"Address."

"All right, all right."

She gave it to him.

Keys began moving at once on his end. Quick. Efficient. Familiar.

After a few seconds, his voice returned.

"Baron Marek Vezhin, deceased last week. Family property wrapped in a trust older than most of the district. Private security, heavy insurance, heritage disputes, a very expensive temperament. You know all that."

"Yes."

"You always ask for things you already know."

"I ask to see whether you know them."

"That is a hostile work environment."

Her gaze drifted across the basement. A row of tall shelves stood where they had stood a moment earlier. Another seemed slightly nearer than before, though she could not have proved it.

"Basement infrastructure. Heating routes. Service pipes. Original utility plans."

The tapping stopped.

Then, quieter:

"There isn't a basement."

She said nothing.

He went on.

"The original house burned down. Major fire. Full loss. The place that stands there now is a later build. Modern facade, clean lines, too severe for the street. The neighbors hated it. Apparently they still do."

Her eyes moved slowly across the stone pillars and the old pipes overhead.

"And below ground?"

"Nothing to speak of on file. No cellar. No service basement. No old pipe network. Ground-level service core only."

The pulse behind the wall went on.

Press. Pause. Press.

"How long ago was the fire?"

"Forty-one years."

She looked toward the stairwell.

The route back lay in sight. One clear aisle, then a turn past the linen racks, then the stairs.

"All right."

His voice sharpened a little.

"What is this?"

"Nothing you can help with."

"That usually means the opposite."

She took one step toward the aisle.

A tall shelf stood across it.

She stopped.

It had not scraped into place. It had not groaned, tipped, or dragged. It simply stood there now, loaded with folded dust sheets and cleaning bottles, as though that had always been its proper home.

His voice came back through the line.

"Hello?"

"Stay available."

"For what?"

A wooden case dropped somewhere close by and burst open against the floor. Glass shattered bright across the stone.

He heard that.

"What was that?"

She ended the call.

The screen went black. She slipped the phone into her pocket and moved at once.

The next aisle over was still open. She took it quickly, feet light, shoulders turned just enough to keep her jacket from brushing the shelves. Even hurried, her movements kept their neatness. The basement seemed to resent that.

A hanging dust sheet tore loose and fell across her path. She caught it in both hands, twisted through it, and came out the other side in one clean motion, only to find a supply cart rolling toward her from a crossing lane with no one behind it.

She sidestepped. The cart clipped her hip hard enough to bruise.

She kept going.

The room had begun to lie openly now.

A passage that should have led straight back to the stairs narrowed around a pillar and ended in stacked crates. She cut left. A rack of mops swung outward and forced her to duck. One wooden handle struck her shoulder. Another glanced off the back of her arm. Irritating blows, not serious, each one chosen to delay rather than disable.

Ahead, between two shelves, she caught sight of the stairs.

She ran for them.

The basement answered with movement from both sides. A metal shelf shifted just enough to squeeze the aisle thin. She turned sideways, slipped through, and felt the edge of it drag across her blazer. A small tin fell from somewhere above and bounced off the floor near her shoe. Another clipped her wrist. She ignored it.

The stairs vanished behind a fresh angle of shelving.

She stopped, corrected, chose the next opening.

That was the worst of it. The room never attacked with drama. It kept revising itself one obstacle at a time, each change small enough to question, each delay large enough to matter. The effect was cumulative. Shelves that had looked merely awkward before now seemed arranged by a patient malice.

She passed the old service sink. Turned right. Found the stairs again.

Closer now.

A long wooden case slid from a higher rack and hit the floor ahead of her with a crack that shook the nearby shelves. She skipped it with a quick lift of both feet, landed softly, and pushed on. Another case struck behind her. Then another, as if the basement were trying to herd her rather than strike cleanly.

The stairs were clear.

She reached them and took them fast, one hand finding the banister, the other held close to her side to keep balance neat and tight.

One step. Two. Four.

For a breath she thought she had beaten it.

Then the angle under her feet gave way.

The staircase softened and lengthened beneath her, the steps running together into a polished downward slope. Her footing vanished. She dropped hard onto her backside and slid back into the basement in one humiliating rush of speed, skirted by the banister, heels forward, breath caught somewhere high in her chest.

The descent lasted only a second or two.

Long enough.

Her left hand caught the rail. Her right went inside the blazer and came out with the black glove.

There was nothing graceful in the motion. The leather snagged at two fingers. She forced them through, dragged the glove into place with a clumsy jerk of the wrist, and had it on by the time she reached the bottom.

She landed hard on the stone floor, seated, one palm braced behind her, hair loosened just enough to fall forward over one cheek.

Above, the slope had already become stairs again.

Plain. Respectable. Innocent.

She stayed where she was for one beat of the hidden pulse.

Then she rose.

A faint ache had settled into her hip and shoulder. Her wrist would likely bruise. Dust marked one side of her blazer. Otherwise the basement had gained very little for all its effort.

Across the room, the shelving had changed again.

This time the shift did not hide itself.

The heavy wooden cases and the tall shelves began to align in a slow, deliberate drawing-in, row after row finding new angles, their weight settling with deep timber knocks against stone. One lane closed. Then another. Cases leaned forward on their own edges. A tall shelf near the sink straightened with a dry wooden complaint, as though it were preparing to move.

Preparing to charge.

She looked at the gloved hand hanging at her side.

Forty-one years ago, according to the records, whatever had stood here below ground had already burned once.

The thought steadied her.

"All right."

The basement held its breath.

Then the scene cut cleanly away from her.

Outside, the mansion stood over Saint Casimir Embankment in damp evening stillness, its facade dark against the river and the old streetlamps.

For one brief, impossible instant, the small basement windows along the foundation flashed with a blinding blue light.

Then they went dark again.
